# Short Suits Sizing (46S, 48S, 50S) with 6-Drop System: Measurements, Anthropometrics, Tailoring Standards, and Brand Examples

## Executive Summary

This report consolidates the measurement standards, anthropometric guidance, tailoring norms, and brand practices relevant to Short suits in sizes 46S, 48S, and 50S under the widely used 6-drop system. It is designed for menswear fit technicians, tailoring professionals, buyers, and consumers who need precise, actionable specifications to choose or construct Short suits that fit well with minimal alterations.

Key findings:

- Core body measurements for suits comprise chest, waist, hips, shoulders, sleeve length, and jacket length, taken using standardized methods. For Short suits, length categories and proportional adjustments are anchored to height bands that differ slightly by brand; anthropometric context further informs fit decisions.[^5][^4][^15][^16]
- The 6-drop system means the trouser waist size is jacket size minus six (e.g., 46S jacket with 40 waist trousers). While classic fits often use a 6-drop, trim and extra-trim fits may use 7-inch or 8-inch drops respectively, and some brands use different nesting rules. Tailors should adjust according to the customer’s chest-to-waist ratio.[^4][^6][^10][^9]
- Across brands, Short suit jacket center-back lengths for 46–50 generally cluster around 28–29 inches; sleeve lengths are typically 1–1.5 inches shorter than Regular, though brand practices vary. Suitsupply’s Slim Fit example shows lengths around 29.75–30.5 inches (Regular-length baseline), while xSuit’s Short lengths are 28.0–29.1 inches for 46–50.[^8][^1]
- Anthropometric recommendations place Short suits for men typically under 5′7″ to about 5′9″ depending on the source. Height-weight guidance for sizes 46–50 is broad and varies by brand, underscoring the need to prioritize direct measurements over assumptions.[^5][^2][^11][^17]
- Brand practices diverge in both measurement detail and nesting logic. Indochino’s made-to-measure (MTM) model eliminates off-the-rack constraints by building garments to individual measurements. Suitsupply offers fit guidance and in-store measurements via Size Passport. Kenneth Cole and JoS. A. Bank illustrate different chest-to-waist nesting approaches, while Brooks Brothers publishes fit offsets that clarify how slim or classic cuts relate to chest and waist measures.[^13][^14][^9][^10][^7]

What’s most brand-agnostic and practical to apply today:

- Anchor size selection on chest measurement and length category by height; verify shoulders and sleeve lengths rigorously; and apply drop adjustments at the trouser waist based on individual proportions rather than relying solely on a default 6-drop.[^5][^4][^6]
- For Short suits, expect shorter jacket center-back lengths and sleeve lengths than Regular; when a brand only publishes Regular-length measurements, an approximate Short adjustment is roughly −1.0 to −1.5 inches in length and −1.0 inch in sleeve, subject to brand-specific tolerances.[^1][^8]
- Use anthropometric guidance to select the length category, but prioritize precise body measurements and try-on validation over generalized height–weight bands.[^5][^15][^16][^11]

Information gaps remain: many brands do not publish Short-specific measurement blocks; sleeve-length deltas for Short versus Regular are inconsistent; and EU conversion mappings for Short lengths are rarely explicit. Tailors should confirm measurements directly with suppliers and be ready to alter within safe bounds (e.g., letting out seams 1–2 inches where possible).[^5]

## Scope, Definitions, and Measurement Framework

This report focuses on Short suits (S length) in numeric sizes 46, 48, and 50, integrating the 6-drop system for trouser sizing. It synthesizes measurement definitions, anthropometric references, and professional tailoring standards with brand-specific practices.

Terminology and size designation:

- Jacket size number corresponds to the chest size (inches), and the letter indicates length category (S/Short, R/Regular, L/Long; some brands offer X-Long).[^4][^5]
- EU sizing (e.g., 46, 48, 50) typically maps to US chest sizes (e.g., 46→US 36, 48→US 38, 50→US 40), though Short/Regular/Long distinctions are not consistently mapped across regions.[^5][^12]
- Drop refers to the difference between jacket size and trouser waist size in a suit set; the most common off-the-rack standard is 6 inches.[^6][^4][^10]

To ground the discussion, the following table summarizes measurement definitions and how to take them.

### Table 1. Measurement definitions and how to take them (jacket and pants)

| Measurement | Definition (what it captures) | How to take it | Practical notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chest | Full chest circumference at the fullest point | Wrap tape under armpits and across shoulder blades; snug with room for one finger | Foundation for jacket size; stand naturally[^4][^5] |
| Over-arm | Breadth across chest and upper arms | Measure around the broadest part of shoulders and upper arms | If over-arm exceeds chest by >7″, consider sizing up[^5] |
| Shoulders | Shoulder width across back | Straight line from shoulder seam point to point over a well-fitting shirt | Critical for shoulder fit; avoid stretching tape[^4] |
| Sleeve length | Shoulder to wrist | From shoulder seam down arm to second knuckle; aim for 1/4–1/2″ shirt cuff show | Short suits typically reduce sleeve length; confirm by brand[^4][^1] |
| Jacket length (center back) | Back length from base of neck to hem | Measure straight down the back to the desired hem point | Primary differentiator for Short/Regular/Long; hard to alter[^4][^5] |
| Waist (pants) | Natural waistline | At belly button or 2–3 fingers below, depending on rise; snug not tight | Belt vs. no-belt fit affects tension; be consistent[^4] |
| Hips (pants) | Seat circumference | Tape at widest point of buttocks, level all around | Add ease for movement; 2 fingers under tape[^4] |
| Inseam / Outseam | Inside/outside leg length | Inseam: crotch seam to hem; Outseam: waistband to hem | Tailor prefers outseam; pants can be shortened more easily than lengthened[^4][^5] |

Standards context (ISO 8559-1):

- ISO 8559-1 provides anthropometric definitions and measurement principles for body measurements used in apparel. It guides how to define, take, and apply measurements for size designation and fit mannequin development.[^15]
- Apparel sizing systems rely on these definitions and population data to establish size charts and shape profiles. Tailors and brands use these conventions to create repeatable fits across collections.[^16]

- Short suits generally shorten the jacket center back and sleeve relative to Regular, while preserving shoulder and chest foundations. Brands that specialize in short men emphasize scaled details, shorter inseams, and proportionate rises to avoid bunching or excess fabric at the sleeves and ankles.[^11]

## Professional Tailoring Standards for Slim-Cut Short Suits

Slim-cut Short suits balance a tailored silhouette with practical proportioning. Several traditional benchmarks guide the fit.

Key standards:

- Collar and cuff show: Approximately 3/8″ of shirt collar above the jacket collar and 1/4″ of shirt cuff extending beyond the jacket sleeve—useful checks for sleeve and jacket length balance.[^19]
- Jacket length: The hem should extend slightly past the crotch level in classic proportions. For Short suits, this still holds; the difference is that both jacket length and sleeve are shorter than Regular while maintaining the relationship to torso and cuff show.[^19]
- Shoulder as foundation: The shoulder seam should meet the natural end of the shoulder with a clean line across the back; this is less adjustable than other areas and crucial for a refined look.[^19]
- Movement and drape: Traditional drape allows gentle fullness through the chest so the jacket moves with the wearer, avoiding strain and pull lines. Slim cuts reduce ease but should not compromise structure.[^19]

Alteration feasibility and constraints:

- Pants seams can often be let out by 1–2 inches and taken in as needed; jackets are far more constrained—sleeve and body length alterations are limited, especially with fused constructions or functional cuffs. Prioritize correct length at purchase.[^5]
- Many modern ready-to-wear (RTW) suits, including some performance or fused constructions, discourage non-hem alterations; some brands void warranties if altered beyond hems.[^1]

Specialty brand practices:

- Peter Manning NYC designs clothing from the ground up for shorter men: shorter body and sleeve lengths, shorter inseams starting around 25 inches, proportionate rises, and scaled details (collars, cuffs, pockets). These principles exemplify how Short-suit tailoring can avoid common pain points like “pirate arms” and ankle stacking.[^11]

### Table 6. Slim-cut tailoring benchmarks

| Benchmark | Target | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Collar show | ~3/8″ above jacket collar | Ensures visual balance and proper shirt–jacket layering[^19] |
| Cuff show | ~1/4″ beyond jacket sleeve | Confirms sleeve length and proportion[^19] |
| Jacket length | Slightly past crotch | Classic proportion; for Short suits, maintain the relative balance with shorter absolute length[^19] |
| Shoulder fit | Seam at natural shoulder end | Foundation for drape; difficult to alter[^19] |
| Movement | Smooth drape without pull | Indicates appropriate ease for daily movement[^19] |

These benchmarks are especially useful when interpreting Short-length measurements that differ by brand; they help maintain proportion even when absolute lengths are shortened.

## The 6-Drop System: Mechanics and Fit Implications

Definition and practice:

- The drop is the difference between the jacket size and the trouser waist in a suit set. A 6-drop means waist = jacket size − 6. For example, a 46S jacket is paired with 40 waist trousers.[^6][^4]
- Fit variations: Classic fits commonly use a 6-drop; trim fits often use a 7-drop; extra-trim fits may use an 8-drop. These are generalizations and vary by brand and collection.[^4]
- Chest-to-waist ratio: The 6-drop is designed for statistical averages. Athletic or broader-shouldered builds with smaller waists may require a larger drop or separate sizing for jacket and trousers.[^6][^4][^10]

Practical implications:

- If a customer’s chest and waist do not conform to the brand’s default drop, choose the jacket size based on chest and shoulders, and select trouser waist separately or alter within safe seam allowances. Branded nesting tables simplify choices when they align with the customer’s proportions; otherwise, mixing sizes is appropriate.[^5][^9]

### Table 7. 6-drop system examples (46S, 48S, 50S)

| Jacket Size | Drop | Trouser Waist |
|---|---|---|
| 46S | 6 | 40 |
| 48S | 6 | 42 |
| 50S | 6 | 44 |

The examples in Table 7 reflect the arithmetic of the 6-drop system. Tailors must still verify waist and rise to ensure comfort and silhouette integrity.[^6][^4][^10]

### Table 8. Drop by fit (general guidance)

| Fit | Typical Drop | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Classic | 6 | Standard RTW baseline[^4][^10] |
| Trim | 7 | Slimmer through waist; verify seat/hip fit[^4] |
| Extra-trim | 8 | Aggressive taper; measure carefully[^4] |

These guidelines help when comparing brands that do not explicitly publish drop values for each collection. Use them as starting points and defer to actual measurements.

## Practical Recommendations and Alterations for Short Suits

A disciplined selection and fitting process reduces alterations and improves comfort and appearance.

Selection criteria:

- Start with chest measurement to determine jacket size; use height bands to choose Short versus Regular/Long; then verify shoulders and sleeve lengths for proportion.[^5][^4]
- Assess the customer’s chest-to-waist ratio to apply the appropriate drop or mix jacket and trouser sizes. Classic fits often use a 6-drop; slim fits may require 7 or 8. Adjust trouser waist and rise accordingly.[^4][^6][^10]

Short suit adjustments:

- Sleeve length: Aim for 1/4–1/2 inch cuff show. If a brand’s sleeve is too long for Short proportions, consider shortening within safe limits; functional cuff constraints may apply.[^4][^19]
- Jacket length: Maintain the classic proportion of slightly past the crotch; Short suits achieve this with shorter absolute lengths. Avoid overly short hems that compromise visual balance.[^19]
- Pants: Use inseam and rise to prevent pooling at the ankles; shorter inseams (e.g., starting at 25″) are helpful for shorter men. Most suits allow letting out seams 1–2″ and taking in as needed.[^5][^11]

Alteration constraints:

- Jackets are difficult and expensive to lengthen; sleeve adjustments are limited by cuff construction and button placement. Fused suits may have strict alteration policies; some brands void warranties if altered beyond hemming.[^5][^1]
- Confirm with the brand or supplier before committing to significant alterations, especially in performance or fused fabrics.[^1]

Brand-specific fit strategies:

- Short-first brands (Peter Manning NYC) prioritize shorter bodies and sleeves, shorter inseams, and proportionate rises—useful design references when choosing off-the-rack or commissioning MTM.[^11]
- MTM approaches (Indochino) eliminate RTW constraints by building to exact measurements; Suitsupply’s Size Passport can capture precise dimensions for future orders and alterations.[^14][^13]

### Table 16. Alteration feasibility matrix

| Garment Area | Typical Feasibility | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Jacket length | Low–Moderate | Often difficult/expensive; prioritize correct length at purchase[^5] |
| Sleeve length | Moderate | Functional cuffs and fused constructions limit changes[^5][^1] |
| Trouser waist | High | Seams often let out 1–2″; take-in straightforward[^5] |
| Trouser inseam | High | Shortening is routine; ensure proper break[^5] |
| Trouser rise | Moderate | Adjustments require skilled tailoring; confirm pattern constraints[^4] |

The matrix clarifies where tailoring efforts yield the most benefit with the least risk: focus on the trousers and minor sleeve tweaks, while selecting jackets with correct foundational lengths.

- The Cloudfront suits chart provides broad height–weight guidance but lacks granular anthropometric tables; interpret with caution.
- Brand fit terminology (e.g., “slim,” “modern,” “tailored”) varies; offsets are not universally published, requiring cross-checking at the style level.
